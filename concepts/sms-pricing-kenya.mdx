If you've ever tried to compare bulk SMS prices in Kenya, you've
probably noticed the same thing every buyer notices: providers quote a
single number on the homepage ("from KES 0.60!") but the actual bill
at the end of the month never quite matches. That's because the
quoted price hides three things — the **per-account rate** your
provider gave you, the **message encoding**, and the **destination
network**.

This page explains how Xyra Bulk SMS prices accounts, the formula you
need to estimate a campaign before you press send, and how to lock in
a sharper rate as you grow.

## How bulk SMS pricing actually works

Independent of which provider you choose, every Kenyan bulk SMS bill
is driven by the same three factors.

### 1. Per-segment, not per-message

The SMS standard splits long messages into **segments** of either
160 characters (GSM-7 encoding) or 70 characters (Unicode/UCS-2
encoding). You are billed **per segment**, not per "message" as a
human would understand it.

A single emoji turns the whole message Unicode, which cuts the segment
length from 160 → 70 characters and roughly **doubles your bill**.
Most "surprise bills" in Kenya come from a single 😊 in a campaign of
50,000 messages. See [Encoding & Length](/concepts/encoding) for the
full character-counting rules.

### 2. Per-account negotiated rate

Your per-SMS rate is fixed on your account once it's provisioned. It
doesn't change automatically with each top-up the way a self-serve
platform's tiers do — it changes when we agree to change it (usually
in a single conversation once your volume justifies it).

This matters because:

- Your finance team always knows the unit cost without checking which
  package is currently active.
- You don't need to commit upfront to a large credit purchase just to
  unlock a better rate.
- Volume that grows over the quarter still earns you the better rate
  going forward, by adjustment on the account.

### 3. Network and sender-type effects

The per-SMS rate on your account is a **blended** price across
Safaricom, Airtel, and Telkom. The carriers themselves charge
different wholesale rates, but you pay the same blended price
regardless of which carrier the recipient is on. This matters because:

- **Campaigns skewed to Safaricom subscribers** (≈65% of the Kenyan
  market) don't get worse pricing.
- **Campaigns skewed to Airtel or Telkom** still benefit from the
  blended rate.

Behind the scenes the carriers also apply different fees for:

- **Alphanumeric sender IDs** (e.g. `XYRA TECH`) vs numeric shortcodes
- **Promotional vs transactional** message classification
- **Time-of-day restrictions** (some carriers throttle promo traffic
  outside 08:00–21:00)

Xyra handles all of these for you under one account rate.

## What a real campaign costs

Let's run through three realistic scenarios at an example rate of
**KES 0.55 per SMS** (mid-volume account).

### Scenario 1 — School fees reminder

A primary school in Kiambu wants to send a fee-balance reminder to
**1,200 parents**.

- Message: `"Dear Parent, this is a reminder that the term 2 fees balance for [Student] is KES [Amount]. Please clear by 30/06. Thank you."` — 142 characters, fits in **1 GSM-7 segment**.
- Cost: **1,200 × KES 0.55 = KES 660**.

### Scenario 2 — SACCO dividend announcement (Swahili)

A SACCO with **8,500 members** sends a Swahili dividend notice
containing the word "shíkílía" (with an accented character).

- That one accented character forces **Unicode encoding**. The
  message is 88 characters, which means **2 Unicode segments**.
- Cost: **8,500 × 2 segments × KES 0.55 = KES 9,350**.

If they had stripped the accent and stayed in GSM-7, the same campaign
would have cost **KES 4,675** — half the price. Always check encoding
before a large send.

### Scenario 3 — E-commerce OTP volume

A Kenyan fintech sends **~50,000 transactional OTPs per month**, each
under 80 characters in plain ASCII (1 GSM-7 segment). At this volume
they'd typically be quoted closer to **KES 0.45 per SMS**:

- Monthly cost: **50,000 × KES 0.45 = KES 22,500**.

For predictable high-volume traffic like OTPs, get the rate quoted up
front — it's worth a 10-minute call before integration.

## Estimating your monthly bill

The formula you'll use most often:

```text
monthly_cost (KES) = recipients × segments_per_message × your_account_rate
```

Quick rules of thumb:

- **Plain English/Swahili, under 160 chars** → 1 segment.
- **With emoji, accents, or Arabic/Hindi script** → start at 2 segments per 70 chars.
- **Long-form messages over 160 chars (GSM-7) or 70 chars (Unicode)** → divide and round up.

## What's NOT charged

To make budgeting clean, the Xyra Bulk SMS API does **not** charge for:

- API calls themselves (only successful submissions to the carrier)
- Delivery-report polling via [/getdlr](/api-reference/get-delivery-report)
- Balance checks via [/getbalance](/api-reference/get-balance)
- Network-status checks via [/mnostatus](/api-reference/network-status)
- Sender ID registration (handled by our team within 2–5 business days)
- Account setup, onboarding, or dashboard access
- Monthly platform / subscription fees

You pay only for SMS segments actually accepted by the destination
carrier. Messages that fail validation (invalid number format, blocked
sender, etc.) are **not** billed.

<Note>
  Messages that the carrier accepts but later marks `UNDELIV` (handset
  off for 48h, number disconnected, etc.) **are** billed, because the
  carrier has already charged us for the submission attempt. This is
  industry-standard across every Kenyan provider.
</Note>
